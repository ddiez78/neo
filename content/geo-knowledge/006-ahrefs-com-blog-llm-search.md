When you search on the internet, there’s a good chance LLMs are involved somewhere in the process.

If you want any chance of visibility in LLM search, you need to understand how to make your brand visible in AI answers.

The latest wave of experts claim to know the “secret” to AI visibility, but the reality is we’re all still figuring it out as we go.

Here is what we _do_ know so far, based on ongoing research and experimentation.

LLM search refers to how large language models gather and deliver information to users—whether that’s via Google’s AI Overviews, ChatGPT, or Perplexity.

Where search engines hand you a list of options, an LLM goes straight to producing a natural language response.

Sometimes that response is based on what the model already knows, other times it leans on external sources of information like up-to-date web results.

That second case is what we call **LLM search**—when the model actively fetches new information, often from cached web pages or live search indices, using a process known as retrieval-augmented generation (RAG).

[](https://ahrefs.com/blog/llm-search/)
## [](https://ahrefs.com/blog/llm-search/)How does LLM search compare to traditional search?

Like traditional search, LLM search is becoming an ecosystem in its own right—only the end goal is a little different.

Traditional search was about ranking web pages higher in search results.

LLM search is about ensuring that your brand and content are discoverable and extractable in AI-generated answers.

| Characteristic | Traditional search | LLM search |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Main goal | Help people find the most relevant web pages. | Give people a straight answer in natural language, backed by relevant sources. |
| Answers you get | A list of links, snippets, ads, and sometimes panels with quick facts. | A written response, often with short explanations or a few cited/mentioned sources. |
| Where answers come from | A constantly updated index of the web. | A mix of the model’s training data and info retrieved from search engines. |
| How fresh it is | Very fresh—new pages are crawled and indexed all the time. | Not as fresh—Retrieves cached versions of web pages, but mostly current. |
| Query composition | Short-tail, intent-ambiguous keyword queries. | Conversational, ultra-long-tail queries. |
| What happens to traffic | Pushes users toward websites, generating clicks. | Intent often met inside the answer, meaning fewer clicks. |
| Ways to influence | SEO best practices: keywords, backlinks, site speed, structured data,etc. | Being a trusted source the model might cite: mentions and links from authority sites, fresh, well-structured, and accessible content, etc. |

## [](https://ahrefs.com/blog/llm-search/)How to optimize for LLM search

AI companies don’t reveal how LLMs select sources, so it’s hard to know how to influence their outputs.

Here’s what we’ve learned about LLM optimization so far, based on primary and third-party LLM search studies.

### [](https://ahrefs.com/blog/llm-search/)1. Boost your brand’s presence on third party websites

We studied 75,000 brands across millions of AI Overviews, and found that [branded web mentions correlated](https://ahrefs.com/blog/ai-overview-brand-correlation/) most strongly with brand mentions in AI Overviews.

### 2. Structure content to please LLMs—without losing readers

A lot of advice has been flying around about structuring content for AI and LLM search—not all of it substantiated.

Personally, I’ve been cautious in giving advice on this topic, because it’s not something we’ve had a chance to study yet.

Which is why Dan Petrovic’s recent article on how [Chrome and Google’s AI handle embedding](https://dejan.ai/blog/inside-chromes-semantic-engine-a-technical-analysis-of-history-embeddings/) was such a welcome addition to the conversation.

Here’s what we took from it.

#### [](https://ahrefs.com/blog/llm-search/)Write “BLUF” content—Bottom Line Up Front

Chrome only ever considers the first 30 passages of a page for embeddings.

That means you need to make sure your most important content appears early. Don’t waste valuable passage slots on boilerplate, fluff, or weak intros.

Also, a very long article won’t keep generating endless passages—there’s a ceiling.

If you want coverage across multiple subtopics, create separate focused articles rather than one massive piece that risks being cut off midstream.

#### [](https://ahrefs.com/blog/llm-search/)Organize your content logically

Google’s AI uses a “Tree-walking algorithm”, meaning it follows the exact semantic HTML structure of a webpage from top to bottom—which is why well-formatted and structured content is easier for it to process.

Organize your content logically—with clear headings, subheadings, and bulleted lists.

![Image 8: Side-by-side comparison showing HTML heading structure examples. Left side labeled "Hard to skim" shows improper heading hierarchy with h3, h2, then h1. Right side labeled "Easy to skim" shows proper hierarchy with h1, h2, then h3. Both examples include placeholder text lines.](https://ahrefs.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/word-image-191075-8.jpg)
I’m sure you’ve been doing this already anyway!

#### [](https://ahrefs.com/blog/llm-search/)Keep content tight—there’s no need to “chunk”

LLMs break content into smaller “passages” (chunks) for embedding.

According to [Dan Petrovic’s findings](https://dejan.ai/blog/inside-chromes-semantic-engine-a-technical-analysis-of-history-embeddings/), Chrome uses a “DocumentChunker Algorithm”, which only analyzes 200-word passages.

**What this means:** structure matters—each section is likely to be retrieved in isolation.

**What this doesn’t mean:** “[chunking](https://ahrefs.com/blog/seo-chunk-optimization/)” is the answer.

You don’t need to make sure every section of your content works as its own standalone idea just _in case_ it gets cited.

And you definitely don’t need to write articles like a series of status updates—that’s not something a user wants to read.

Instead logically group paragraphs, and develop ideas cleanly—so that they make sense even if they get spliced.

![Image 9: Side-by-side comparison of two webpage layouts showing content structure differences. The left layout (marked with red X) shows mixed content blocks with blue headers, light blue text sections, green highlighted areas, and orange sections scattered throughout in a less organized manner. The right layout (marked with green checkmark) displays a more structured approach with blue headers at the top, followed by organized green content blocks, then blue sections, and orange content at the bottom, demonstrating better visual hierarchy and organization.](https://ahrefs.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/word-image-191075-9.png)
Avoid long, rambling sections that might get cut off or split inefficiently.

Also, don’t force redundancy in your writing—AI systems can handle overlap.

For example, Chrome uses the `overlap_passages` parameter to make sure that important context isn’t lost across chunk boundaries.

So, focus on natural flow rather than repeating yourself to “bridge” sections—overlap is already built in.

### [](https://ahrefs.com/blog/llm-search/)3. Build long-tail keyword content clusters

Building content clusters and targeting niche user questions may increase your odds of being surfaced in an AI response.

Our [AI Overview research](https://ahrefs.com/blog/insights-from-56-million-ai-overviews/) shows that user prompts in AI are longer and more complex than those in traditional search.

![Image 10: Line graph comparing "AIO distribution vs. Normal search distribution by Word count." Shows two lines (blue for Non-AIO, red for AIO) plotting percentage against word count from 1-10+. AIO peaks at 3 words (24.96%) while Non-AIO peaks at 3 words (21.61%).](https://ahrefs.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/word-image-191075-10.png)
In AI assistants like ChatGPT and Gemini, prompts skew ultra long-tail.

Growth Marketing Manager at [AppSamurai](https://appsamurai.com/), [Metehan Yeşilyurt](https://metehan.ai/blog/i-analyzed-1827-real-user-prompts-from-chatgpt-here-what-ive-found-agentic-search/), studied ~1,800 real ChatGPT conversations, and found the average prompt length came in at 42 words(!).

And long-tail prompts only multiply.

AI assistants essentially “fan out” prompts into numerous long-tail sub-queries. Then, they run those sub-queries through search engines to find the best sources to cite.

Targeting long-tail keywords can therefore increase your odds of matching intent and winning citations.

You can get long-tail keyword ideas by performing a competitor gap analysis in Ahrefs Brand Radar.

This shows you the prompts your competitors are visible for that you’re not—your AI prompt gap, if you will.

Drop in your brand and competitors, hover over an AI assistant like ChatGPT, and click on “Others only”.

### 4. Optimize content for commands—not just questions

All of the problems that Google couldn’t solve are now being handed over to AI.

LLM search can handle multi-step tasks, multi-modal content, and reasoning, making it pretty formidable for task assistance.

Going back to the ChatGPT research mentioned earlier, Metehan Yeşilyurt found that [75% of AI prompts](https://metehan.ai/blog/i-analyzed-1827-real-user-prompts-from-chatgpt-here-what-ive-found-agentic-search/) are commands—not questions.

This suggests that a significant number of users are turning to AI for task completion.

In response, you may want to start **action mapping**: considering all the possible tasks your customers will want to complete that may in some way involve your brand or its products.

To map customer tasks, head to [Ahrefs Competitor Analysis](https://ahrefs.com/site-explorer) and set up a search to see where your competitors are visible–but you’re not.

![Image 15: Screenshot of Ahrefs Competitive Analysis setup page. Then filter by relevant action keywords (e.g. “make”, “track”, “create”, “generate”) and question keywords (e.g. “how to” or “how can”).

### 5. Keep content fresh

We analyzed 17 million citations across 7 AI search platforms, and found that [AI assistants prefer citing fresher content](https://ahrefs.com/blog/do-ai-assistants-prefer-to-cite-fresh-content/).

Content cited in AI is 25.7% fresher than content in organic SERPs, and AI assistants show a 13.1% preference for more recently _updated_ content.

ChatGPT and Perplexity in particular prioritize newer pages, and tend to order their citations from newest to oldest.

Why does freshness matter so much? Because RAG (retrieval-augmented generation) usually kicks in when a query requires fresh information.

If the model already “knows” the answer from its training data, it doesn’t need to search.

But when it doesn’t know, it looks for the most recent information available—especially when it comes to emerging subjects.

In the example below, Hubspot pushed an update to a blog on “small business ideas” on April 9th, based on Content Changes data in [Ahrefs Site Explorer](https://ahrefs.com/site-explorer).

![Image 17: Screenshot of Ahrefs Site Explorer showing organic traffic performance for blog.hubspot.com/sales/small-business-ideas. The graph displays a dramatic drop in organic traffic around April 9th 2025 (marked as "9th April update"), falling from around 200K+ monthly visits to under 50K, followed by a recovery to about 150-200K by August 2025.](https://ahrefs.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/word-image-191075-17.png)
The article is now their most cited blog in AI Overviews, according to Ahrefs Brand Radar. That single content update has driven 1,135 new AI Overview mentions.

![Image 18: Screenshot of Ahrefs Brand Radar showing a "Cited pages" report with filtering options. Our research suggests that keeping your content updated can increase its appeal to AI engines looking for the latest information.

### 6. Make sure AI crawlers can access your site

For your content to be cited in AI answers, you need to allow AI bots to crawl it.

A growing number of sites have started blocking AI scrapers.

Going by our own research, [~5.9% of all websites disallow OpenAI’s GPTBot](https://ahrefs.com/blog/ai-bot-block-rates/) over concerns about data use or resource strain.

![Image 19: Horizontal bar chart titled "AI Bots blocked (%)" showing blocking percentages for various AI crawlers and bots. All bars appear to be roughly the same length (around 6% on the scale), indicating similar blocking rates across different AI bots including GPTBot, CCBot, Amazonbot, Bytespider, ClaudeBot, Google-Extended, Anthropic-AI, FacebookBot, and many others. The chart lists approximately 20 different AI bots with consistent blocking percentages across all entries.](https://ahrefs.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/word-image-191075-19.png)
While that’s understandable, blocking might also mean forfeiting future AI visibility.

If your goal is to have ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini and other AI assistants mention your brand, double-check your robots.txt and firewall rules to make sure you’re not accidentally blocking major AI crawlers.

Make sure you let the legitimate bots index your pages.

This way, your content can be part of the training or live browsing data that AI assistants draw on—giving you a shot at being cited when relevant queries come up.

You can check which AI bots are accessing your site by checking your server logs, or using a tool like [Cloudflare AI audit.](https://developers.cloudflare.com/ai-audit/)

### 7. Diversify your marketing strategy for different platforms

The top-cited domains vary a lot between different LLM search surfaces. Being a winner in one doesn’t guarantee presence in others.

In fact, among the top 50 [most-mentioned domains](https://ahrefs.com/blog/top-mentioned-sources-are-not-shared-across-ai-assistants/) across Google AI Overviews, ChatGPT, and Perplexity, we found that only 7 domains appeared on all three lists.

![Image 21:  Three-circle Venn diagram titled "Overlap of the Top 50 most cited brands in AI Assistants by count." Shows Google AI Overviews (orange circle, 19 unique), ChatGPT (blue circle, 35 unique), and Perplexity (green circle, 16 unique) with overlapping sections showing shared citations: 1 brand cited by all three, 7 brands shared between all pairs, and various other intersection counts.](https://ahrefs.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/word-image-191075-21.jpg)
That means a staggering 86% of the sources were unique to each assistant.

**Google** leans on its own ecosystem (e.g. YouTube), plus user-generated content—especially communities like Reddit and Quora.

**ChatGPT** favors publishers and media partnerships—particularly news outlets like Reuters and AP—over Reddit or Quora.

And**Perplexity** prioritizes diverse sources, especially global and niche sites—e.g. health or region-specific sites like tuasaude or alodokter.

There’s no one-size-fits-all citation strategy. Each AI assistant surfaces content from different sites.

If you only optimize for Google rankings, you might dominate in AI Overviews but have less of a presence in ChatGPT.

On the flip side, if your brand is picked up in news/media it might show up in ChatGPT answers—even if its Google rankings lag.

In other words, it’s worth testing different strategies for different LLMs.

### [](https://ahrefs.com/blog/llm-search/)Final thoughts

A lot of this advice may sound familiar—because it’s largely just SEO and brand marketing.

The same factors that drive SEO—authority, relevance, freshness, and accessibility—are also what make brands visible to AI assistants.

And tons of recent developments just prove it: ChatGPT has recently been outed for [scraping Google’s search results](https://www.theinformation.com/articles/openai-challenging-google-using-search-data), GPT-5 is [leaning heavily](https://dejan.ai/blog/gpt-5-made-seo-irreplaceable/) on search rather than stored knowledge, and LLMs are buying up [search engine link graph data](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pNzZnbGBkGI) to help **weight**and **prioritize**their responses.

By that measure, SEO is very much not dead—in fact it’s doing a lot of the heavy lifting.

So, the takeaway is: double down on proven SEO and brand-building practices if you also want AI visibility.

Generate high-quality brand mentions, create structured and relevant content, keep it fresh, and make sure it can be crawled.

As LLM search matures, we’re confident these core principles will keep you visible.
