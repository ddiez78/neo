## Key Takeaways

*   A large-scale Ahrefs study of 863,000 keywords and 4 million AI Overview URLs found that**only 38% of pages cited in Google AI Overviews also rank in the top 10**for the same query — down sharply from 76% just seven months earlier.
*   A separate BrightEdge analysis puts the top-10 overlap even lower, at approximately**17%**, depending on dataset and methodology.
*   The remaining citations are divided nearly equally between pages ranking positions 11–100 (31.2%) and pages that do not appear in the top 100 at all (31%).
*   **YouTube is now the single most-cited domain**in Google AI Overviews and accounts for 18.2% of all citations that come from outside the top 100.
*   Google’s upgrade to**Gemini 3 as the global default for AI Overviews on January 27, 2026**, is a likely contributing factor to the shift in citation behavior.
*   The underlying mechanism driving these changes is Google’s**query fan-out process**, which splits a user’s original query into multiple sub-queries and draws from across all of those sub-query results.
*   Organic CTR for queries with AI Overviews has fallen by as much as**61%**, making citation visibility an increasingly important alternative metric to track.
*   The SEO implication is clear:**ranking #1 for a single keyword no longer guarantees AI Overview visibility**. Topical depth, multi-format content, and fan-out query coverage are now the primary levers.

## Why Ranking #1 Is No Longer Enough

For years, the correlation between organic search rankings and AI Overview citations was close enough that SEOs could treat one as a proxy for the other. If a page ranked at the top of the SERP for a given query, it had a high probability of being cited when Google’s AI Overviews appeared for that same query. That relationship has changed materially.

In mid-2025, approximately three out of four pages cited in an AI Overview also ranked in the top 10 organic results for the same keyword. By early 2026, that figure stands at roughly one in three — or possibly as low as one in six, depending on the data source you reference. This is not a minor statistical fluctuation. It reflects a structural shift in how Google’s AI selects and attributes source material.

For any business, content team, or SEO professional that built their AI visibility strategy around the assumption that organic rankings automatically translate into AI citations, this data demands a strategic recalibration. This post breaks down what the research shows, why the shift occurred, what YouTube’s emerging dominance means, and what specific steps you can take right now to protect and expand your visibility in Google’s AI Overviews.

## The Research: What the Data Actually Shows

### The Ahrefs Study (863,000 Keywords, 4 Million URLs)

Ahrefs published an updated analysis of AI Overview citations drawn from 863,000 keyword SERPs and approximately 4 million AI Overview URLs sourced from its Brand Radar tool. This dataset is more than double the size of Ahrefs’ previous study conducted in July 2025, which analyzed roughly 1.9 million citations across a smaller keyword set.

The headline finding is this:**38% of URLs cited in AI Overviews also appeared within the first 10 result blocks of the SERP**for the exact same query. When Ahrefs filtered to standard organic listings only — removing ads, featured snippets, People Also Ask boxes, and video packs — the share of top-10 cited pages dropped slightly further to 37.1%.

The remaining citations break down as follows across the organic-only dataset:

*   **Rank 1–10:**37.1% of cited URLs
*   **Rank 11–100:**26.2% of cited URLs
*   **Outside top 100:**36.7% of cited URLs

The near-equal distribution between the 11–100 group and the beyond-100 group is significant. It means that roughly two in three AI Overview citations come from pages that a user searching that specific keyword would never encounter on page one of the SERP. The AI is not primarily drawing from what the user sees. It is drawing from a much broader pool.

### The BrightEdge Findings

A separate analysis from BrightEdge, published February 12, 2026, reported a top-10 overlap for AI Overview citations of approximately**17%**. The two studies use different methodologies and datasets, so they are not directly comparable. However, both point in the same direction: the relationship between top-10 organic rankings and AI Overview citation selection has weakened substantially in a short period.

### The Historical Trajectory

To appreciate how significant this change is, consider the timeline of data points:

**Late 2024:**Multiple studies showed that roughly**75% of AI Overview citations came from pages ranking in the top 10 to 12 organic positions**. At this stage, the conventional SEO wisdom — that ranking well for a query was effectively sufficient to earn AI Overview visibility — was broadly supported by the data.

**July 2025 (Ahrefs):**A prior Ahrefs study of 1.9 million citations found that**76% of cited pages ranked in the top 10**for the same query, with the median ranking position for top-cited pages sitting at position 4. The consensus was still that traditional SEO and AI Overview optimization were largely the same exercise.

**October 2025 (BrightEdge):**BrightEdge reported a**54% overlap**between AI Overview citations and organic top-10 rankings — a notable decline from earlier in the year, but still a majority.

**February 2026 (Ahrefs and BrightEdge):**The most recent data puts the overlap between**17% and 38%**, representing a drop of approximately half or more from six months prior.

The pace of change here is what commands attention. In the span of roughly 18 months, the top-10 overlap figure has gone from around 75–76% down to somewhere in the 17–38% range. That is not a gradual drift. It is a meaningful behavioral shift in how Google’s AI selects sources.

## Why This Is Happening: Query Fan-Out and Gemini 3

### Understanding Google’s Query Fan-Out Process

The most likely explanation for the widening gap between organic rankings and AI Overview citations is the**query fan-out process**— a mechanism that Google has confirmed is central to how AI Overviews are constructed.

When a user enters a search query and Google’s AI is triggered, the system does not simply evaluate the top-ranking pages for that one query. Instead, it**decomposes the original query into multiple related sub-queries**. These sub-queries cover adjacent topics, related entities, and alternative phrasings that a user might not have typed but that are likely relevant to their underlying informational need.

Google then evaluates the search results across all of those sub-queries and identifies the pages that appear most frequently and most authoritatively across the full set. Those pages become the cited sources in the AI Overview.

The practical consequence is that the source pool for any given AI Overview response is far larger than the top 10 results for the original query. A page that ranks position 40 for a closely related sub-query may end up cited in an AI Overview triggered by the primary query, even though that page would never appear in the top results for the primary query itself.

Google has publicly documented the existence of the fan-out process in its developer documentation. What the Ahrefs data suggests is that**fan-out queries may now play a larger role in source selection than they did in mid-2025**— possibly because Gemini 3 performs more aggressive or broader query expansion than earlier model versions.

SEO consultant Ethan Lazuk describes the practical implication directly: the goal is no longer to optimize content for individual keywords but for entire user journeys, with fan-out queries guiding what “comprehensive coverage” actually means in practice.

### The Gemini 3 Upgrade

On January 27, 2026, Google made Gemini 3 the global default model powering AI Overviews across all markets. The timing correlates with the period when Ahrefs observed the citation behavior shift.

SE Ranking’s post-upgrade analysis found that**Gemini 3 replaced approximately 42% of the domains that had previously been cited**in AI Overviews under the earlier model, and that Gemini 3 delivers roughly 32% more source URLs per AI Overview response than its predecessor. A larger number of sources per response, combined with a wider source pool drawn through more aggressive fan-out behavior, would mathematically reduce the percentage of citations coming from any single tier of organic results — including the top 10.

Ahrefs explicitly flags the Gemini 3 upgrade as additional context for the timing of the citation shift, while noting that Google has not publicly confirmed any specific changes to how fan-out behavior is weighted in the new model.

### Improved Detection Methodology

Ahrefs also acknowledges one caveat that affects how the two studies should be compared: its parsing methodology has improved since the July 2025 study, enabling it to detect more of the citations that appear in AI Overviews. This means a portion of the apparent drop in top-10 overlap may reflect better measurement of citations that were previously missed, not purely a change in Google’s selection behavior. The direction of change is not in doubt, but the exact magnitude requires the caveat.

## YouTube’s Emergence as a Top Citation Source

One of the most actionable findings in the Ahrefs data is the role YouTube now plays in Google’s AI Overview citation behavior.

Among AI Overview citations that came from pages**not ranking in Google’s top 100 organic results**,**18.2% were YouTube URLs**. Across the full dataset of all AI Overview citations, YouTube URLs accounted for**5.6% of the total**. According to Ahrefs Brand Radar data, YouTube is currently**the most-cited domain in AI Overviews overall**and has grown its citation share by**34% over the past six months**.

This is not an isolated finding from one study. Multiple independent analyses have reached similar conclusions:

*   An SE Ranking study of German health-related search queries found that YouTube was the most-cited domain in health AI Overviews, appearing more frequently than official medical institutions or hospital websites.
*   In Ahrefs’ December 2025 AI Mode comparison study, YouTube was already the most frequently cited source for AI Overviews specifically.
*   An OtterlyAI study published in March 2026, drawn from over 100 million citation instances, identified YouTube as the second most-cited social platform in AI search citations.
*   A Semrush citation study tracking the most-cited domains in AI tools found YouTube’s share of citations growing sharply across both AI Overviews and other AI search platforms.

Ahrefs’ separate research on 75,000 brands found that**mentions of a brand on YouTube — specifically in video titles, transcripts, and descriptions — represent the strongest correlating factor with AI Overview visibility**among all the signals studied. This puts YouTube content in a category of its own: it drives AI Overview citation probability more than most other signals tested.

The mechanism is consistent with query fan-out logic. YouTube videos frequently rank for the sub-queries that Google generates when performing fan-out from the original search query, even when those videos do not appear in the top 100 for the original query itself. Google’s AI then pulls from those YouTube URLs as authoritative sources for the expanded topic set.

## What This Means for Traffic and Visibility

### The CTR Compression Effect

The shift in citation sources cannot be evaluated in isolation from what AI Overviews are doing to click-through rates more broadly.

Seer Interactive’s September 2025 study, reported by Search Engine Land, found that**organic CTR for queries with AI Overviews dropped from 1.76% to 0.61%**— a 65% decline. Paid CTR for the same queries fell from 19.7% to 6.34%, a 68% drop.

Ahrefs’ own December 2025 update found that AI Overviews**reduce the organic CTR for position-one content by 58%**. For the keyword that a page ranks first for, adding an AI Overview to the SERP roughly cuts click traffic in half.

The implication of these two trends together is stark. As AI Overviews appear more frequently — seoClarity found their presence on U.S. mobile searches grew approximately**475% year-over-year between September 2024 and September 2025**— organic click traffic is being compressed at the same time that organic rankings are becoming a less reliable path to earning AI Overview citation visibility.

Being cited in an AI Overview, rather than just ranking organically, now matters more as a visibility metric. A page cited in an AI Overview gains a form of AI-mediated visibility even in a SERP where the user may not click through to any standard organic listing. That brand exposure and topical authority signal has value beyond the immediate click.

### The 97% Top-20 Finding

It is important not to read the Ahrefs 38% figure as meaning organic SEO has become irrelevant to AI Overview citations. SeoClarity’s analysis of 432,000 keywords found that**97% of AI Overviews cite at least one source from the top 20 organic results**for the query. Position 1 pages appear in AI Overviews more than 50% of the time. Organic ranking remains a meaningful factor — it is just no longer sufficient on its own.

The two findings coexist: ranking well improves citation probability, but it does not guarantee citation selection. And many citations come from pages that don’t rank in the top 10 for the original query at all.

## What SEOs and Content Teams Need to Do Now

The data points toward a set of practical adjustments that can meaningfully improve AI Overview citation probability. These are not speculative tactics — each is grounded in the patterns the research has identified.

### 1. Shift from Single-Keyword Optimization to Topic-Cluster Architecture

If AI Overviews are pulling from the results of multiple fan-out sub-queries, then the most effective way to improve citation probability is to cover a topic from multiple related angles. A single well-ranked page targeting one primary keyword competes for citation in one SERP. A cluster of well-structured pages covering a topic’s adjacent questions, sub-topics, and entity relationships competes for citation across the full set of sub-query SERPs that fan-out generates.

In practice, this means building content hubs where a pillar page covers the primary topic comprehensively and supporting pages address specific sub-questions, entities, and intent variants. Each supporting page has an independent chance of appearing in a fan-out sub-query SERP, and that citation feeds into the AI Overview for the primary query.

Keywords Explorer parent topic clustering tools help identify which related queries are likely to be treated as thematically connected by Google’s AI, providing a starting point for mapping the sub-query landscape.

### 2. Reframe What “Comprehensive Content” Means

Content length alone has essentially no correlation with AI Overview citation frequency. Ahrefs’ analysis found that 53% of all AI Overview citations go to pages under 1,000 words. The question is not how long a piece of content is but whether it addresses the specific aspects of a topic that Google’s fan-out process considers relevant.

What matters is whether the content explicitly answers the likely sub-queries generated when the primary topic is expanded. This requires understanding the full user journey around a topic — not just the search intent of one query, but the informational territory that a person researching that topic might need to traverse. Content that answers multiple related questions within a well-structured, easily parsed format tends to perform better in citation selection than long-form content that covers one angle in depth.

### 3. Build a YouTube Presence — Treat It as an SEO Asset

Given that YouTube is now the most-cited domain in Google AI Overviews and that mentions in YouTube video titles, transcripts, and descriptions correlate most strongly with AI Overview visibility, the case for treating YouTube as part of an SEO strategy — not just a social media or brand-building channel — has become data-supported.

For businesses that currently produce no video content, this does not require a major production operation. Repurposing existing content into structured video explanations, tutorials, or expert commentary — with carefully crafted titles and accurate auto-transcriptions — creates YouTube content that can compete for AI citation in fan-out sub-query SERPs.

The mechanism is this: when Google expands a user’s query into sub-queries during fan-out, YouTube videos frequently rank in those sub-query SERPs, particularly for informational and how-to intent. Those videos then get pulled into the AI Overview for the primary query, even if they would never rank organically for that primary query term.

For any brand seeking to build AI Overview visibility, YouTube content is now a concrete, measurable lever — one that Ahrefs’ research on 75,000 brands has empirically validated.

### 4. Replicate Fan-Out Queries in Your Content Planning Process

Since Google doesn’t expose the sub-queries it generates through fan-out, content teams need to simulate them. Several tools and workflows have been developed by the SEO community to help with this:

**Qforia**, built by iPullRank founder Mike King, allows users to generate fan-out query expansions for any given topic.**GoFishDigital’s Gemini API and Screaming Frog workflow**enables extraction of AI Overview query fan-outs at scale. The**WordLift AI Visibility Fan-Out tool**provides a collaborative research interface for the same purpose.

The goal is not to perfectly replicate Google’s internal fan-out queries — those are probabilistic and change with each run. The goal is to analyze enough expansion examples to identify the recurring themes, entity relationships, and intent angles that Google’s AI consistently considers adjacent to the primary topic. Those recurring patterns then become the basis for the content architecture: each identified angle represents a topic that should have dedicated, high-quality content coverage on the site.

### 5. Implement Structured Data With Precision

Research from SE Ranking found that approximately 65% of pages cited by Google AI Mode include structured data markup, and roughly 71% of pages cited by ChatGPT carry some form of structured data. Wellows’ analysis of AI Overview ranking factors attributed a 73% selection boost to structured data implementation.

The schema types with the clearest relevance to AI Overview citation include: Article, FAQPage, HowTo, Q&A, SpeakableSpecification, VideoObject, and Organization. FAQPage schema is particularly valuable for directly answering the kinds of questions that fan-out sub-queries tend to generate. JSON-LD implementation is Google’s recommended format, and it can be audited for correctness through Google’s Rich Results Test tool.

Structured data serves multiple purposes in the AI citation context. It makes content easier for Google’s AI to parse accurately, signals the format and purpose of the content explicitly, and creates machine-readable connections between entities that support topical authority assessment.

### 6. Optimize for E-E-A-T at the Page and Domain Level

Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness (E-E-A-T) remain central to how Google evaluates sources for AI Overview citation. Expert author attribution with verifiable credentials, accurate citations of data and primary sources, clear publication and review dates, and a track record of accurate information all contribute to the trust signals that influence whether a page gets selected.

Recent Ahrefs research found that expert quotes in content can increase AI Overview citation probability relatively quickly. For YMYL (Your Money or Your Life) topics — health, finance, legal — this trust signal is particularly important.

### 7. Audit and Track Your AI Overview Citation Performance

Understanding which of your pages are currently being cited in AI Overviews, and for which queries, is foundational to improving your citation rate. You cannot optimize what you cannot measure.

Google Search Console provides some AI Overview impression data, though it is limited. Third-party tools like Ahrefs Brand Radar, SE Ranking’s AI Overview tracker, BrightEdge’s Generative Parser, and SEMrush’s AI Overviews tracking give more granular visibility into citation patterns by domain and topic.

The analysis framework should include: which of your pages are already being cited (revealing topics where you have established authority); which queries trigger AI Overviews in your category but do not cite your pages (revealing gaps); which competitor pages are being cited instead of yours for those queries (revealing what additional coverage or format changes might close the gap); and which pages rank well organically but are not being cited (suggesting topical coverage or clarity issues that prevent selection despite ranking).

### 8. Diversify Content Formats

The Ahrefs data shows that different content formats get cited for different query types. Definitions and direct answers tend to appear in AI Overviews for informational queries. Step-by-step instructions and how-to content gets cited for procedural queries. Data, statistics, and comparative tables get cited in research-oriented queries. Video content, as discussed, is particularly effective for informational and tutorial content types.

For any given topic cluster, producing content in at least two or three formats — a written guide, a video explainer, and a data-rich reference page — increases the surface area across which fan-out sub-query SERPs can find and cite your content.

## How to Measure Whether Your Strategy Is Working

The key performance indicators for AI Overview visibility differ from traditional organic SEO metrics. The metrics that matter now are:

**AI Overview impressions by page and query:**Available in Google Search Console with limited granularity, and in greater detail through enterprise SEO platforms. Trending upward means the strategy is working.

**Citation rate by topic cluster:**What percentage of queries in a given topic area where an AI Overview appears are citing at least one of your pages? This is your market share within the AI-visible search landscape for that topic.

**YouTube citation frequency:**Are your brand’s YouTube videos appearing in AI Overview citations? Ahrefs Brand Radar’s “Cited in AI” column enables this analysis directly.

**Organic CTR by AI Overview exposure level:**Segments queries by whether an AI Overview appeared. CTR will typically be lower for AI Overview queries, but being cited should partially offset that decline by generating brand exposure.

**Branded vs. unbranded AI mention share:**For longer-term brand visibility, tracking how often your brand is mentioned in AI Overview text — not just linked in citations — provides a broader measure of AI-mediated authority.
